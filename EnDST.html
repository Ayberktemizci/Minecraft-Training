Purpose of This Library

This section contains a real, recorded Kodland Level 1 session evaluated by the Senior Quality Assurance team. Study each timestamped annotation carefully — not to memorise scripts, but to internalise the reasoning behind each instructional decision. A tutor who understands why a behaviour is effective can adapt it to any unexpected classroom situation; a tutor who only mimics surface actions will struggle the moment the lesson departs from the script.

Watch the video, then read the annotation cards beneath it. Each card identifies the exact moment, describes what the tutor did, and explains the pedagogical principle that makes it work — or the gap that needs correction.

This session ran longer than ideal, but the core discipline of resolving completely before teaching is correct and must be preserved in every future session.

QA Evaluation Framework — How Every Session Is Assessed

The Five Assessment Dimensions

Every Kodland session is evaluated against five dimensions. Understanding them helps you self-assess in real time during lessons.

Dimension 1 — Timing Discipline

Pre-Flight Check complete by minute five · Intro ceiling respected (20 minutes maximum) · Mandatory screen-off break delivered at minute 30 · Independent practice occupies minutes 35–55 · Reflection delivered in the final five minutes. A session that violates any timing guardrail is flagged regardless of content quality.

Dimension 2 — Pedagogical Alignment

Co-Explorer framing used consistently · Error normalisation applied to all student mistakes · Specific, behaviour-linked feedback used (not generic praise) · Active involvement confirmed at key checkpoints · Fast finishers redirected to prepared extension tasks immediately.

Dimension 3 — Technical Accuracy

Concepts taught match the module's objectives · Explanations are correct — no technical errors passed as facts to students · Correct terminology used throughout.

Dimension 4 — Student Engagement

All students attempt every task independently · No student is idle for more than two minutes · The tutor does not complete tasks on behalf of students · Engagement is verified, not assumed.

Dimension 5 — Accountability Closure

Reflection questions asked at end of session · Homework assigned with specific success criteria · Screenshot or output requested with a concrete deadline · Parent communication channel used where applicable.

Common Failure Patterns — Know These Before Your First Live Session

  • Intro overrun: Spending more than 20 minutes explaining before students build. The most common failure. Result: students disengage and the session ends without independent practice.
  • Missing screen-off break: Skipping the break at minute 30 to "catch up." Result: student attention collapses in minutes 35–50, negating the time saved.
  • Solving instead of hinting: Completing the task for a student instead of guiding them. Result: the student cannot replicate the work independently in the next session.
  • Generic praise: "Good job" without specificity. Result: zero reinforcement — the student has no information about which behaviour to repeat.
  • No extension tasks prepared: Fast finishers sit idle. Result: disruption and wasted capacity in your most capable students.
4 · Digital Skill Test — Readiness Assessment

What the Test Assesses

The Digital Skill Test is the formal readiness gate that determines whether you are ready to lead a live Minecraft Level 1 group independently. It measures two dimensions:

  • Hard Skills (10 questions): Technical knowledge across all five modules — coordinates, Redstone mechanics, MakeCode blocks, Hopper mechanics, Agent commands, and more.
  • Pedagogy and Soft Skills (10 questions): Application of teaching frameworks — the 60-minute session structure, the mandatory break, error normalisation, active involvement techniques, specific praise, and cooperative multiplayer management.
⚠️
Pass mark: 80% (16 of 20). This is higher than the module knowledge checks because the Digital Skill Test is the final professional gate before independent teaching. It requires demonstrated mastery across all dimensions, not just general familiarity.
